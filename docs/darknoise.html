Why the default payment doesn't need Lightning — or liquidity, channels, or anything else you have to manage.

The default payment

Alice pays Bob $20 in a chat

t + 0 s — send her wallet selects notes, derives nullifiers, ML-KEM-encrypts the bundle to Bob's scan key, ships it to the relay mailbox, submits the 64-byte record. All local.
▼
t + seconds — visible Bob's chat shows the $20 bubble (Nostr is instant), and the record hits Bitcoin's mempool, which his client watches — the practical assurance that ran a decade of 0-conf retail. Asleep? It waits in his mailbox — receivers need zero liveness and zero setup: no channel, no inbound liquidity, no UTXO, no bitcoin.
▼
t + minutes — proven the STARK follows asynchronously (~1 min laptop CPU today; seconds delegated to a GPU prover that never holds spend keys). Bob verifies it in milliseconds.
▼
t + ~1 block — trustless the record lands in a Bitcoin checkpoint; Bob's assurance upgrades from "bonded relay" to "proof-of-work," automatically.

The core move is splitting authorization from settlement — the shape of every payment system people actually like. Cards authorize in a second and settle in days; Darknoise authorizes in a heartbeat and settles in minutes-to-a-block, while the conversation moves on.

The deletion list

What Lightning charges for the default case

Lightning collapses authorization and settlement into one instant trustless event. Elegant — and here is its bill, every line of which Darknoise deletes:

  • Inbound liquidity — acquired, rented, or begged before you can receive at all
  • Channel opens — an on-chain transaction per user before the first payment
  • Splicing and rebalancing — ongoing capital management as balances drift
  • Force-close risk — funds hostage to counterparty cooperation and fee spikes
  • Liveness or watchtowers — go offline wrong and lose money
  • LSP mediation — in practice, a company managing all of the above for you

Direct transfers aren't routed, so liquidity doesn't exist as a concept. The honest line item on our side: between mempool and confirmation the assurance is practical, not cryptographic — fine for $20 among humans; for $2M the wallet says "one block"; channels give instant-trustless to regulars; and a fully-designed optional speed layer (bonded sub-second receipts) stays shelved until stranger-retail demands it.

Channels came back — but on our terms. Bitcoin never got eltoo because its script can't say "highest co-signed state wins"; our kernel just says it, in Rust. No penalty algebra, no toxic state, safe backups, pure hashes — and XOR-chained per-hop preimages give the payment decorrelation PTLCs were invented for. Channels become an optimization for relationships, not the network's fabric: nobody else ever notices they exist.

The doorway

Lightning as a neighbor

Lightning HTLCs settle by revealing a SHA-256 preimage — pure hash cryptography, already our primitive, already post-quantum. So a note spendable by preimage (refundable after timeout) makes gateway swaps atomic: lock UV-USDT to a Lightning invoice's payment hash, the gateway pays the invoice, and the settlement preimage is the gateway's claim key. If the gateway never pays, the timeout refunds. Custody-free, competitive RFQ market, exposure bounded per swap — classical crypto only on Lightning's side of the door, which is Lightning's rail, not ours.

The Lightning question's final form

Operate no liquidity network, keep channels for friends, and keep one hash-shaped door open.
