The thesis

Off-chain, size stops mattering

Client-side validation keeps signatures and proofs out of Bitcoin blocks — so the block-space economics that make Bitcoin's own post-quantum migration slow and contentious simply don't apply. A 7.9 KB hash-based signature is unpayable on-chain and a rounding error off-chain. Ultraviolet spends that freedom on the most conservative cryptography that exists: hash-based signatures, hash-based recursive STARKs, hash commitments, hash-derived nullifiers. The chain contributes the one thing a quantum computer cannot forge anyway — proof-of-work ordering.

Neither RGB v0.12 (Schnorr-bound ownership) nor Blockstream's Shielded CSV (Schnorr nullifiers) makes this claim. Ultraviolet is the synthesis: Shielded CSV's architecture, RGB's contract generality, a hash-only assumption base.

The whole system is three nouns

1 · NOTES

The money

A note is a private hash commitment to (asset, amount, owner, randomness), living only on its owner's devices. Owners are post-quantum hash-based keys. Nobody else ever sees a note — not relays, not the chain, not even the sender after it's spent.

2 · RECORDS

The double-spend lock

Spending publishes a keyless 64-byte record on Bitcoin; first occurrence wins. That is the chain's entire job — no UTXO owned, no script run, zero UTXO-set growth, no soft fork. Receive with an empty Bitcoin wallet.

3 · PROOFS

The validity

Every transfer carries one recursive STARK proving its entire history — constant size at any depth (1,242 KB, measured). An asset that moved two million times verifies as fast as one that moved twice. Hashes only; no trusted setup.

Everything else is these three composed over today's Nostr network, unmodified — zero new server software. Payments are visible in seconds (the record hits Bitcoin's mempool) and final in about a block; channels are co-signed note-splits for instant-trustless with regulars; the client is a chat app that moves notes. A fully-designed optional speed layer (bonded sub-second receipts) stays shelved until a market demands it.

Architecture

One transfer, three layers

Sender wallet spends a note with a hash-based signature; extends the recursive STARK; encrypts the new note to the receiver's ML-KEM scan key (non-interactive — no invoice, no liveness)
▼ proof bundle (~60–320 KB, encrypted)
Nostr + Blossom events on ordinary Nostr relays; proof bundles on Blossom blob servers. Replicated, permissionless, existing. Seed + relays = full wallet recovery. Confidentiality never depends on the store.
▼ 64 bytes: nf ‖ H(transfer bundle)
Bitcoin orders keyless nullifier records in OP_RETURN. First occurrence of a note's nullifier wins — double-spend and equivocation prevention with nothing on-chain for a quantum computer to attack.

The client

Payments are messages: Blacklight

The client is a chat app. Blacklight forks White Noise — the audited, Rust-core Nostr messenger (MLS groups via the Marmot protocol) — and makes "send $20" a message bubble in an existing conversation. Your npub stays your social identity; money answers only to SLH-DSA-signed address records that ride Nostr as events. The relays are today's ordinary Nostr relays doing what they already do — storage and mailbox delivery — carrying event kinds they don't need to understand. Records go to Bitcoin from the client itself: self-published, receiver-published, or cooperatively batched.

Payments have two states: visible (the record in Bitcoin's mempool, seconds) and final (confirmed, about a block) — wallets pick their comfort by amount, and channels give instant-trustless to regulars. Events to the relay, KEM to the human, MLS if you keep talking, Bitcoin for finality. The full no-Lightning story — and why channels came back kernel-native anyway — is Darknoise.

The UTXO set, untouched

OP_RETURN outputs never enter the UTXO set — millions of Ultraviolet transfers add zero entries to the state every full node carries. ~64 weight units per transfer versus ~560 for an average transaction: roughly 10× the settlement density, before batching. And because ownership never binds to an output, a quantum thief who steals the anchoring UTXO steals nothing.

Saying it precisely

“Quantum-safe,” earned

  • Theft & forgery: quantum-safe under hash assumptions alone — the conservative extreme of the PQ spectrum, NIST's own fallback if lattices fall.
  • Confidentiality: quantum-safe under NIST lattice assumptions (hybrid ML-KEM-768 + X25519 — no practical hash-based KEM exists). A lattice break would erode privacy, never funds. The hybrid defends against harvest-now-decrypt-later today.
  • Ordering: depends on Bitcoin's economic health through its own migration (BIP-360/361) — systemic, not cryptographic. Never say “quantum-proof.”

Case study

UTEXO & native USDT: the requirements document

Tether's UTEXO venture is shipping native USDT on Bitcoin over RGB v0.11.1 + Lightning right now — proof the market wants CSV assets, and a preview of what breaks at scale. A high-velocity stablecoin accrues history fastest exactly where adoption succeeds most: on RGB every receive replays it; on Ultraviolet receive stays O(1). And the USDT mint key — the single most valuable quantum target in the ecosystem — is a classical key on RGB. Here it is SLH-DSA from day one: your mint key is hash-based, today.

Where the incumbent leads, honestly: UTEXO's Lightning channels deliver instant settlement now; post-quantum channel constructions (PQ adaptor signatures) are open research. Coexistence, then convergence.

Honest accounting

Open problems

  • Two review gates before anything holds value: an external security audit, and adversarial review of the channel dispute rules.
  • Proving is ~1 minute per hop on a laptop CPU; proofs are 1,242 KB against a 50–300 KB target — GPU/delegated proving and FRI tightening are engineering, not research.
  • Spend authorization is host-side in v0; SLH-DSA verification moves in-circuit next.
  • No trustless bridge for BTC itself — v1 scope is issued assets, where issuance is native.

The complete, authoritative list is spec/99 — if it isn't there, it isn't open.
